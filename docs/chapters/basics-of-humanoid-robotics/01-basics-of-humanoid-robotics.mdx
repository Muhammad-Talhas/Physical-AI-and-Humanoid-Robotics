## 2.1 Introduction to Humanoid Robotics

Humanoid robots are robots designed with a human-like body structure, typically featuring a head, torso, two arms, and two legs. The field encompasses both the mechanical design aspects and the intelligent control systems needed to achieve human-like capabilities.

### Why Humanoid Robots?

Humanoid robots are designed for several key reasons:

- **Environmental Compatibility**: Human-shaped robots can operate in human-designed environments
- **Social Interaction**: Human-like form facilitates natural human-robot interaction
- **Research**: Understanding human locomotion and behavior through robotics
- **Practical Applications**: Assisting humans in various tasks

## 2.2 Anatomy of a Humanoid Robot

A humanoid robot consists of several interconnected subsystems:

### Mechanical Structure
- **Frame/Chassis**: The structural foundation
- **Joints**: Actuated degrees of freedom mimicking human joints
- **Limbs**: Arms, legs, head, and torso segments
- **End Effectors**: Hands and feet designed for manipulation and locomotion

### Actuation Systems
- **Servo Motors**: Precise position control for joints
- **Hydraulic Systems**: High-power actuation for larger robots
- **Pneumatic Systems**: Compliant actuation for safe human interaction
- **Series Elastic Actuators**: Variable stiffness for safe and natural movement

### Sensory Systems
- **Vision Systems**: Cameras for visual perception
- **Tactile Sensors**: Touch and pressure sensing
- **Proprioceptive Sensors**: Joint position, velocity, and torque feedback
- **IMU**: Inertial measurement units for balance and orientation
- **Force/Torque Sensors**: For interaction with environment

## 2.3 Kinematics and Dynamics of Humanoid Robots

### Forward Kinematics
Calculating the position and orientation of the robot's end effectors based on joint angles.

### Inverse Kinematics
Determining the joint angles required to achieve desired end effector positions.

### Balance and Locomotion
- **Zero Moment Point (ZMP)**: Stability criterion for bipedal walking
- **Capture Point**: Extension of ZMP theory for dynamic balance
- **Center of Mass (CoM)**: Critical for maintaining stability

## 2.4 Degrees of Freedom and Mobility

Humanoid robots typically have 20-30+ degrees of freedom (DOF) to achieve human-like mobility:

### Upper Body DOF
- **Head**: 2-3 DOF for gaze and expression
- **Arms**: 7+ DOF per arm (shoulder: 3, elbow: 1, wrist: 3)
- **Hands**: 10-20 DOF depending on sophistication

### Lower Body DOF
- **Hips**: 3 DOF per leg
- **Knees**: 1 DOF per leg
- **Ankles**: 2 DOF per foot
- **Torso**: 3-6 DOF for upper body movement

```python
class HumanoidDOF:
    def __init__(self):
        self.head = 3  # yaw, pitch, roll
        self.left_arm = 7  # shoulder(3), elbow(1), wrist(3)
        self.right_arm = 7
        self.left_leg = 6  # hip(3), knee(1), ankle(2)
        self.right_leg = 6
        self.torso = 3  # waist(3)

    def total_dof(self):
        return (self.head + self.left_arm + self.right_arm +
                self.left_leg + self.right_leg + self.torso)
```

## 2.5 Control Architectures for Humanoid Robots

### Hierarchical Control Structure
- **High-Level Planning**: Task planning and sequencing
- **Mid-Level Control**: Trajectory generation and gait planning
- **Low-Level Control**: Joint servo control and stabilization

### Control Strategies
- **PID Control**: Proportional-Integral-Derivative for joint control
- **Impedance Control**: Controlling interaction forces
- **Admittance Control**: Controlling motion in response to forces
- **Learning-Based Control**: Adaptive control through experience

## 2.6 Types of Humanoid Robots

### By Size and Purpose
- **Small Humanoids**: Educational and research platforms (e.g., NAO, Pepper)
- **Adult-Size Humanoids**: Research and demonstration (e.g., ASIMO, HRP series)
- **Full-Size Humanoids**: Industrial and service applications

### By Mobility
- **Static Humanoids**: Limited to standing positions
- **Walking Humanoids**: Bipedal locomotion capability
- **Dynamic Humanoids**: Running, jumping, and complex movements

## 2.7 Challenges in Humanoid Robotics

### Technical Challenges
- **Balance and Stability**: Maintaining stability during complex movements
- **Power Management**: Efficient energy use for extended operation
- **Real-time Control**: Meeting strict timing requirements for stable control
- **Mechanical Design**: Achieving human-like dexterity and mobility

### Social and Ethical Challenges
- **Uncanny Valley**: Avoiding unsettling reactions to human-like robots
- **Safety**: Ensuring safe human-robot interaction
- **Acceptance**: Overcoming cultural and social barriers
